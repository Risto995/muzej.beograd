BODENEHR'S ALBUM (FIG. 17)
PLAN OF BUDIM (AND PEST)

Plan of Budim and Pest with a mark of N-S orientation in the upper central part. Castle, town and suburbs or the coastal town are schematically represented, along with Pest on the left bank of Danube . In a short description and history it is erroneously noted that the town (Ofen) had a Latin name Buda (Budim), that it lies on Danube and that it is placed 32 miles (cca. 250 km) away from Vienna. Along with an approval of its healthful environment it is noted that across the river, on the opposite Danube bank, existed another town - Pest. History begins with a note that Suleiman I the Magnificent conquered Budim in 1526. On that occasion, German inhabitants were released, and the Hungarians (men, women and children) were smothered. Subsequently, in 1527, archduke Ferdinand (German Emperor since 1556) captured it, but Suleiman seized it again in 1529 and gave it to the son of Jovan Zapolja (Szapolyai Janos) until 1541 when he has taken it over personally. Unsuccessful Christian sieges of Budim are enumerated: 1542, 1598, 1599, 1602, and 1684. Finally after 145 years of Turkish government Budim was liberated on 2 June 1686. Remains of besiege works of 1686 could be seen in representation of the large semi-circle around Budim. On the right side is an explanation with entries A-E, and at the top of plan is the number 131.
